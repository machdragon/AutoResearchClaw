   (tang2024survey, huang2024compression)

## Cluster 1 – General Transformer/LLM Compression Landscape

**Core idea:**  
Provides the conceptual and empirical backdrop tying model compression to performance, but at a coarse granularity.

**Representative works:**  
- **tang2024survey** – taxonomy of pruning, quantization, distillation, architectural tweaks; discusses metrics like model size, FLOPs, latency, accuracy.  
- **huang2024compression** – empirically shows a near-linear relationship between compression ability (bits-per-character / bits-per-byte) of LLMs and downstream benchmark performance.

**Relevance to lane_6 (byte-grouped serialization for artifact compression):**

- Establishes that **compression efficiency measured via val_bpb is a meaningful capability proxy**, but it does **not touch on weight file / artifact encoding formats**.
- The survey stresses **multi-objective optimization**: accuracy, latency, memory; but does not address **how the *serialized representation* of quantized weights interacts with these metrics**.
- huang2024compression uses **text compression** as a probe of LLM ability; lane_6 is about **model weight compression** (artifact), where val_bpb is a performance metric on external text. Bridging these two “compressions” is conceptually interesting but unexplored.

**Synthesis points:**

- You can reasonably **treat val_bpb as a sensitive probe** for changes to weight storage/packing schemes: if your packing scheme degrades the model, huang2024compression suggests val_bpb will move consistently with capability.
- However, **none of the works consider low-level byte packing of weights as a compression axis**; compression methods are mostly logical/algorithmic (quantization, sparsity), not **I/O-level or serialization-level**.

## Cluster 2 – Quantization, Calibration, and Robustness

**Core idea:**  
How to get and keep good accuracy with low-bit weights under non-ideal deployment conditions.

**Representative works:**  
- **campos2024qcore (QCore)** – continual calibration for quantized models using a tiny representative data subset and a bit-flipping network; no full backprop.  
- **yun2025starting** – demonstrates that **initialization strongly impacts quantization robustness**; introduces GHN-based parameter prediction to produce weights that remain accurate after post-training quantization.

**Relevance to lane_6:**

- Lane_6 assumes a **quantized weight regime** (you serialize “quantized weights with byte-grouped packing”).  
- QCore and GHN-QAT show that:
  - There is a **non-trivial sensitivity** of task accuracy to details of quantization and bit manipulations.
  - Lightweight **bit-level changes to quantized parameters** (QCore’s bit-flips) or **initial parameter structure** (GHNs) can significantly affect performance.
- Neither paper considers **artifact size or serialization format** directly, but their results imply that **any re-encoding of quantized weights must preserve bit patterns exactly** (or at least semantics) if you want to avoid hurting post-roundtrip val_bpb.

**Synthesis points:**

- For lane_6, **the risk surface is not the pack/unpack CPU overhead per se**, but:
  - potential **alignment / rounding or padding changes** that could subtly affect which bits are stored or how they’re interpreted;
  - interaction between **packing scheme and future bit-level adaptation methods** (e.g., QCore) — your format must remain amenable to local bit updates.
- QCore’s streaming calibration requirement indirectly speaks to the **quick-gate runtime constraint (≤ 1.10×)**: model-side changes should stay lean; extra overhead per call (e.g., complex decompression) is tightly constrained at the edge.
