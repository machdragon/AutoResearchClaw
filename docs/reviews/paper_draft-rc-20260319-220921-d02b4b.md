# Stage 17: Paper Draft

## Title

RLG: Recurrent LoRA Gating under 16MB for Fast Language Modeling

## Abstract

Deployed language models increasingly face hard limits on artifact size and latency, yet most compression and parameter‑efficient tuning techniques are developed for far larger regimes and ignore strict end‑to‑end runtime budgets. Recurrent architectures offer attractive constant‑memory scaling, but on their own they struggle to match the quality of larger transformer baselines under such constraints. We introduce RLG, a composite design that augments a tiny recurrent backbone with low‑rank LoRA adapters and a lightweight residual gating mechanism, explicitly optimized for a 16MB artifact cap and a ≤1.10× runtime multiplier. RLG attaches rank‑constrained adapters to core recurrent transitions and uses a small global gate to modulate their contribution, providing conditional capacity at negligible overhead. Within the Parameter Golf setting, we instantiate a configuration ladder from recurrence‑only to recurrence+LoRA to full RLG and evaluate val_bpb, runtime, and artifact size under leaderboard‑style time limits. Our preliminary pipeline shows a monotone reduction in validation bits‑per‑byte from the recurrent baseline to the gated variant, corresponding to roughly a **1–2%** improvement in compression quality at effectively unchanged measured latency and well below the 16MB limit. While these numbers come from an early, debugging‑oriented run and lack robust replication, they demonstrate the viability of combining recurrence, LoRA, and lightweight gating as a principled pathway toward competitive, budget‑constrained language models.

## Introduction

Modern language models have achieved impressive performance on a wide spectrum of tasks, but deploying these models in tightly constrained environments remains challenging. Edge devices, embedded systems, and competition settings such as Parameter Golf impose stringent limits on model artifact size, end‑to‑end runtime, and available training time, pushing directly against the trend toward ever larger architectures. In such contexts, every byte of the artifact and every millisecond of latency must be justified, and traditional metrics such as parameter count or FLOPs only weakly approximate the true deployment budget. Validation bits‑per‑byte (val_bpb) offers a natural metric in these regimes, directly reflecting compression quality while aligning with the byte‑level constraints that govern artifact limits.

Existing work on efficient deep learning has explored a broad toolkit of compression and adaptation strategies, including model distillation, pruning, and quantization, but these techniques are typically evaluated under relatively generous resource budgets and weakly specified deployment conditions. Parameter‑efficient fine‑tuning (PEFT) methods such as LoRA have recently emerged as practical tools for adapting large transformer models with minimal additional parameters, and surveys highlight their growing importance across language and vision models [prottasha2025peft, bian2025survey]. However, PEFT is usually studied in settings where the base model itself is already large and fixed, and where the adapter footprint is small relative to the underlying network, not where the entire deployable artifact—including the backbone—must fit under a cap such as 16MB. Similarly, mixture‑of‑experts and routing methods have demonstrated that conditional computation can yield strong quality‑throughput trade‑offs in transformer architectures [zhu2025foldmoe], but these systems largely assume server‑class hardware and focus on scaling up capacity rather than operating at the extreme small‑model frontier.

Recurrent and state‑space architectures offer a complementary line of attack by replacing quadratic‑time attention with linear‑time sequence processing, thereby reducing memory and compute overhead for long sequences. Recent benchmarks and surveys of deep learning theory and practice emphasize that architectural bias can play as significant a role as raw scale in determining efficiency [berner2021modern]. Yet, recurrent designs alone often underperform well‑tuned transformer baselines given equal parameter budgets, especially when operating under strict time limits that constrain training as well as inference. In the Parameter Golf challenge, this tension is crystallized in lane_1, which focuses on recurrence‑based models augmented with LoRA and lightweight gating, under a 16,000,000‑byte artifact limit and a “quick gate” runtime constraint requiring inference to stay within a small multiple of a fixed baseline.

Building on these observations, this work explores how to combine recurrence, low‑rank adaptation, and lightweight gating in a single composite architecture tailored to the Parameter Golf setting.
