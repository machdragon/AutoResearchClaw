# Stage 17: Paper Draft

## Title

ZQAT: Codec-Roundtrip Quantization-Aware Training for Parameter-Golf Compression

## Abstract

Deploying neural sequence models under strict memory and latency gates requires compression strategies that optimize exactly what runs in production rather than an idealized floating-point surrogate. Existing quantization-aware training and post-training quantization techniques typically match float and low-precision predictions but ignore the concrete deployment codec, leading to a mismatch between training objectives and the post-roundtrip behavior of compressed models. ZQAT introduces codec-roundtrip quantization-aware training tailored to the Parameter Golf setting, where models must fit within a 16MB int8+zlib artifact and respect a ≤1.10× runtime quick-gate while minimizing validation bits-per-byte. The key idea is a staged QAT schedule that intermittently routes weights through the actual deployment pipeline—int8 quantization, zlib compression, decompression, and int8 inference—under a fixed gate budget of codec calls, aligning optimization with post-roundtrip `val_bpb`. Within this framework, ZQAT is designed to be directly comparable to strong float+PTQ and codec-agnostic staged-QAT baselines sharing the same training and gate budgets. Once populated with experimental results, the abstract will report at most three concrete numbers, such as a **relative reduction in post-roundtrip `val_bpb`** under the artifact and runtime caps and the corresponding change in compressed model size. This codec-aligned perspective aims to make compression metrics first-class optimization targets in constrained deployments.

## Introduction

Modern neural language and sequence models increasingly operate under hard deployment constraints, where memory footprint, storage format, and runtime latency are as important as raw predictive accuracy. Embedded devices, edge servers, and leaderboard-style benchmarks all impose tight gates that limit both the size of deployable artifacts and the training and inference budget allocated to any given model. In such environments, compression performance is not an incidental byproduct of a well-regularized float model; it is the primary metric that determines viability. The Parameter Golf challenge crystallizes this setting by requiring participants to produce models that minimize validation bits-per-byte (val_bpb) while obeying a strict 16,000,000 byte artifact limit, a quick-gate constraint of at most 1.10× baseline runtime, and short train/eval budgets on standardized hardware. These rules transform compression-aware training from an optional refinement into a central algorithmic objective.

Despite the prominence of quantization-aware training (QAT) and post-training quantization (PTQ) in model compression, most existing techniques optimize a proxy objective that stops short of the actual deployment stack. Standard PTQ calibrates a float model and then applies per-tensor or per-channel quantization to minimize some notion of reconstruction or task error, while QAT injects fake quantization operations during training so the model learns to tolerate quantization noise [tang2024survey, hasan2024optimizing, chen2025scaling]. These approaches have enabled aggressive bit-width reductions, including low-bit attention and mixed-precision pipelines [zhang2026attnqat, liang2025gplq, maskey2026wonder, ke2025dlqat]. However, they almost always assume that the quantized weights are used directly at inference, neglecting the fact that real deployments often serialize and compress these weights using general-purpose codecs such as zlib. When performance is ultimately measured after a full roundtrip through quantization and lossless compression, this omission can induce a misalignment between the training objective and the deployed behavior.

Compression-oriented work has begun to close this gap by treating compressibility as a first-class objective. Research on model compression and entropy coding for neural networks explores pruning, low-rank factorization, and learned quantization schemes tailored to subsequent entropy coding [ren2023lowrank, jacobellis2024learned, hurwitz2024compression, patel2024compress]. Learned image and video compression systems similarly integrate differentiable entropy models into the training loop and target bits-per-pixel metrics directly [bai2021scalable, ladune2021conditional, mao2023extreme, lei2023text, damodaran2023rqatinr]. Yet most of these methods rely on custom arithmetic coders or neural codecs that are, by design, trainable or at least tunable. By contrast, Parameter Golf’s lane_3 environment fixes the deployment codec to zlib, a non-differentiable, lossless compressor based on LZ77 and Huffman coding that cannot be smoothly integrated into stochastic gradient descent.
