# Stage 17: Paper Draft

## Title
GOLF: Boundary-Aware Compression for 16MB Parameter Golf

## Abstract
Modern parameter‑golf settings require candidate models to meet a strict deployment artifact cap and to pass a fast, standardized quick‑harness evaluation, yet prior compression and PEFT work rarely treats the promotion frontier and harness latency as first‑class constraints. We introduce GOLF, a lightweight automatic controller that jointly optimizes validation bits‑per‑byte (val_bpb), artifact size (hard cap ≤16MB), and a quick‑harness cost proxy by re‑allocating representational bits toward examples near the promotion boundary and high‑risk classes. GOLF implements a boundary‑aware compressor that measures promotion‑distance and applies a small, targeted redundancy budget to boundary regions while compressing the low‑risk bulk; this design is engineered for short, reproducible run budgets and records-folder submission. In the recorded quick‑harness snapshot used for debugging, val_bpb variation was small (clustered within a 0.003 range), and **most runs finished successfully with success_rate = 1.0**, although two ablation variants failed (success_rate = 0.0); these logs motivated unit tests and integrity fixes described in the paper. Together, the method and the engineering checklist enable reproducible, artifact‑aware parameter‑golf experiments and a practicable path to evaluate boundary‑conditioned robustness under strict artifact budgets.

## Introduction
Parameter‑golf is a practical discipline for producing deployable models that meet strict artifact‑size budgets while delivering acceptable predictive performance. In continuous delivery pipelines and constrained device deployments, teams must shrink models until artifacts fit promotion budgets (e.g., a 16MB cap) and simultaneously guarantee that the promoted artifact will not introduce unexpected safety or compliance failures in production. Existing research on model compression and parameter‑efficient training focuses predominantly on global accuracy, parameter counts, or FLOP reductions, leaving a gap at the intersection of (a) artifact‑size constraints, (b) tight per‑candidate wall‑clock budgets used in quick‑harnesses, and (c) promotion‑boundary‑local safety signals that are selectively amplified by aggressive compression. Prior surveys and methods in parameter‑efficient fine‑tuning and PEFT catalog many mechanisms to reduce parameter counts, but they do not treat artifact‑size as a hard promotion gate together with boundary‑local robustness objectives [prottasha2025peft, bian2025survey]. Building on operational needs exposed by continuous compliance tooling [marino2024compliance, schoning2025compliance] and practical quick‑harness benchmarks [banbury2021mlperf], we aim to close this gap.

Compression can interact with model behavior in subtle, non‑uniform ways: some failure modes are induced or amplified near decision boundaries or for high‑risk input classes. Recent work on refusal boundary instability and artifact‑dependent compliance demonstrates that deployment artifact details can materially affect safety behavior, motivating artifact‑aware design choices [heverin2026prompt, hu2025safety]. Meanwhile, frameworks for adaptive compliance and automated continuous compliance show the operational value of lightweight controllers that trade off multiple deployment constraints [hou2024adaptive, angermeir2024automated]. Taken together, these lines of work suggest that an automatic controller which jointly reasons about val_bpb, artifact size, and quick‑harness cost — and which allows directed investment of bits near promotion boundaries — could yield practical benefits for parameter‑golf workflows.

We present GOLF (a short, memorable controller name), an automatic parameter‑golf controller designed to (i) respect a hard 16MB artifact cap at promotion, (ii) operate within short, reproducible quick‑harness budgets, and (iii) allocate redundancy in a boundary‑aware manner to reduce post‑promotion violations that often concentrate near the promotion frontier. GOLF is intentionally simple in its core optimizer: it combines a val_bpb loss, an artifact‑size penalty that is enforced as a hard verification step in the records‑folder pipeline, and a boundary weighting term that increases representational allocation for examples near the promotion decision surface. Critically, GOLF is implemented with small, localized experiment files and unit tests so it can be integrated without sweeping repository refactors and so record submissions remain small and verifiable [canesche2023preparing, ratiu2023defining].
