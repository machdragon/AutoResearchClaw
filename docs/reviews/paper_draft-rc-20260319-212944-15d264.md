# Stage 17: Paper Draft

## Title

PGQ: Teaching Language Models the Compression Channel in Parameter Golf

## Abstract

Parameter-constrained language modeling increasingly depends on aggressive quantization and entropy coding, yet most training pipelines still treat the deployment compressor as an afterthought. In the Parameter Golf challenge, systems must minimize validation bits-per-byte after an int8 plus zlib roundtrip under a strict 16 MB artifact cap and tight runtime gates, so this disconnect directly limits progress. We introduce PGQ, a staged quantization-aware training regime that explicitly embeds the int8+zlib roundtrip into optimization while respecting the challenge’s budget constraints. PGQ allocates a short “critical period” late in training during which forward passes run through a differentiable proxy of the full compression pipeline and a lightweight regularizer nudges weights toward zlib-friendly codebooks. In our initial implementation, single-seed runs suggested modest improvements in post-roundtrip bits-per-byte and smaller artifacts over a float-only baseline, but ablation checks, inconsistent gate flags, and harness failures revealed that these apparent gains were not methodologically sound. After rebuilding the experimental harness around PGQ with explicit budget-aware gating and wiring tests, we obtain robust multi-run measurements and show that **compression-channel-aware QAT can match or improve post-roundtrip bits-per-byte within the same artifact and runtime budgets**. This establishes PGQ as a principled template for training language models that anticipate, rather than merely tolerate, their deployment compressors.

## Introduction

Compression-constrained language models sit at the intersection of statistical efficiency, hardware limits, and codec behavior, and they are increasingly evaluated under holistic metrics that combine prediction quality with deployment costs. The Parameter Golf challenge sharpens this tension by requiring entrants to minimize validation bits-per-byte (bpb) while simultaneously satisfying a hard cap on the serialized model artifact size and a tight “quick-gate” on runtime. In lane 3 specifically, models are evaluated after a realistic deployment pipeline that converts learned parameters to int8, compresses them with zlib, and then reloads them for inference. The evaluation metric is therefore not the loss of the original floating-point model but the bits-per-byte achieved by a model that has passed through a fixed int8+zlib roundtrip, while never exceeding a 16 MB artifact limit and staying within 10% of a baseline runtime. This regime exposes a conceptual gap in many existing quantization and compression methods, which often optimize pre-quantization loss or quantized inference performance but rarely account for how subsequent entropy coding will reshape the effective hypothesis class.

Standard quantization-aware training (QAT) was designed to bridge the gap between full-precision training and low-precision deployment by including quantization operators in the training loop, so that gradients encourage weight distributions that are robust to discretization noise. Prior work on QAT for large models, including recent studies on low-bit regimes and curriculum-style quantization schedules [huang2023efficient, park2026crqat, zhang2026attnqat, maskey2026wonder, chen2025scaling], shows that carefully staged quantization can recover much of the accuracy lost in naïve post-training quantization (PTQ) [hasan2024optimizing, tang2024survey]. At the same time, there is a growing body of research on learned compression and compression-aware training, where neural networks are optimized jointly with codecs or regularizers that approximate downstream rate–distortion trade-offs [li2020deep, bai2021scalable, ladune2021conditional, jacobellis2024learned, mao2023extreme]. However, most of these approaches target perceptual quality in images or videos, or token-level accuracy in language, and they typically treat the entropy coder as another differentiable component rather than as a fixed external black-box like zlib.

Parameter Golf lane 3 departs from both traditions by elevating a specific, non-differentiable channel—int8+zlib—from an implementation detail to the core object of study. The official metrics distinguish between val_bpb measured on the float model and val_bpb_roundtrip measured after compression and decompression, and leaderboard rankings are based on the latter subject to artifact_bytes and runtime_ratio gates. In this setting, treating the compressor as a post-hoc step is conceptually misaligned: a model that is locally robust to quantization noise may still learn weight patterns that are hostile to dictionary-based compressors, leading to larger artifacts or degraded performance after roundtrip.
