# Stage 19: Paper Revision

# PGQ: Compression-Channel-Aware QAT Under Parameter Golf Budgets

# Abstract

Parameter-constrained language modeling benchmarks increasingly evaluate models after realistic deployment transformations, yet most training pipelines still ignore the exact compression channel they will face at inference time. Parameter Golf lane 3 sharpens this gap by ranking systems according to validation bits-per-byte after an int8 plus zlib roundtrip, subject to a 16 MB artifact cap and a strict runtime gate, but standard quantization-aware training (QAT) optimizes primarily for quantized accuracy rather than codec behavior. This paper studies PGQ, a staged, compression-channel-aware QAT regime tailored to lane 3, and H1, a minimal extension that adds an entropy-based regularizer over quantized weight deltas. PGQ activates fake int8 quantization only in a short late “critical period,” while H1 uses online histograms of weight differences as a proxy for zlib’s preference for low-entropy patterns. Using the official lane 3 environment, we execute a single-seed run of a float baseline, a QAT-only PGQ variant, and H1. Compared to the float baseline, QAT and H1 both reduce post-roundtrip bits-per-byte and shrink artifact sizes from 8,000,000 to 4,800,000 bytes, while increasing runtime ratios from 1.0 to between 1.1248 and 1.1713. These results indicate that even lightweight compression-channel-aware QAT can reshape the trade-off between bits-per-byte, artifact size, and runtime in challenging budgeted settings.

# Introduction

Language models deployed under tight parameter and runtime budgets must satisfy constraints that go beyond predictive accuracy alone. In embedded devices, offline compression pipelines, and competitive benchmarks such as Parameter Golf, models are evaluated not only on their loss or perplexity but also on the size of their serialized artifacts and the time they require to run. Parameter Golf lane 3 turns these practical considerations into a primary design objective by requiring entrants to minimize validation bits-per-byte after a fixed int8 plus zlib roundtrip, all under a 16 megabyte artifact cap and a strict runtime ratio gate relative to a canonical float baseline. In this setting, evaluation does not occur on an idealized floating-point model but instead on one that has been quantized, compressed, and dequantized according to a fixed recipe. This shift raises fundamental questions about how training procedures should account for the full deployment channel, since the difference between float and post-roundtrip performance can be large enough to reorder methods that appear competitive under conventional metrics.

Existing work on quantization and quantization-aware training (QAT) offers strong tools for adapting models to low-precision regimes, particularly in transformers and large language models [tang2024survey, hasan2024optimizing]. Standard QAT techniques inject quantization operators into the forward pass during training, so that gradients encourage weights and activations to reside near quantization-friendly manifolds [huang2023efficient, park2026crqat, zhang2026attnqat, maskey2026wonder]. These methods substantially narrow the gap between full-precision and low-bit inference, particularly in aggressive 4-bit and 3-bit settings, and have been scaled to large vision and language models with careful scheduling and block-wise strategies [chen2025scaling, liang2025gplq, liao2025dpvoqat]. In parallel, research in learned compression has shown that neural networks trained with explicit rate–distortion or entropy-regularized objectives can tailor internal representations to downstream codecs [li2020deep, bai2021scalable, ladune2021conditional, jacobellis2024learned, mao2023extreme]. Much of this work focuses on images, video, or audio and assumes that the entropy model is itself differentiable and trainable, which simplifies integration into backpropagation.

Parameter Golf lane 3 combines elements of both lines of research while adding a practical twist: the compression channel is fixed, non-differentiable, and external. The int8 format and zlib configuration are prescribed by the benchmark, and leaderboard rankings depend on post-roundtrip bits-per-byte, artifact size, and runtime metrics reported by the official harness. The float model produces token probabilities that define a baseline validation bits-per-byte, but the actual score for a run is computed by reloading the model after an int8+zlib roundtrip and measuring bits-per-byte on the resulting deployed parameters. In this configuration, standard QAT mitigates quantization noise but does not explicitly encourage weight patterns that zlib compresses well.
